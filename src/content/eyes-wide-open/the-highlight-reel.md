Everyone you follow is showing you their best frame. Not their life, the edit of it. The holiday photo, not the row at the airport that morning. The promotion, not the four rejections before it. The glowing couple, not the silence in the car home. You are watching a film of other people's best moments, cut to look like their ordinary days. You compare it to your own life with the boring bits left in.

That is the trick. It works on everyone, because it runs on something older than any app. You compare. We all do. For most of history you compared yourself to the few dozen people in your village. You came out somewhere in the middle, a survivable place to be. Now you compare yourself to the polished best of millions, all day. The verdict is always the same. Everyone else is winning. You are behind.

The old line is right. Comparison is the thief of joy. It walks in and takes the thing you were content with two minutes ago, because someone on a screen has a better one.

This falls hardest on the people who have never known anything else. A generation grew up with the highlight reel running from childhood, measuring their unfinished, awkward, ordinary teenage lives against a feed engineered to make them feel small. The numbers on their mood and their anxiety are not a mystery. You cannot lose every comparison, every day, for years. Nobody comes out of that whole.

The way out is not a clever mindset. It is distance. You cannot reason your way to peace while the machine that breaks it is in your hand all day. So you put it down. You log off for a weekend and feel the strange quiet, then the relief, then the slow return of your own life at its own size, no longer measured against anyone. The people who seem most at ease are often the ones who stepped out of a game they never agreed to enter.

You do not owe the reel your attention. Put it down for a day and watch what comes back: your own afternoon, unedited, no better or worse than anyone's, yours. That is enough. It always was. The reel made it look small.
