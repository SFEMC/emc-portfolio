There is one question that turns most of the world from a mystery into a machine you can read. Not "is this good or bad", not "what do they say they are doing", but "who gains and what are they rewarded for". Ask it of almost anything and the strange behaviour in front of you starts to make sense.

People act on what pays them, not on what they say and not always on what they believe. A rule tells you what someone is supposed to do. An incentive tells you what they will do when the rule is not looking. When the two pull in opposite directions, the incentive wins. So if you want to predict how a person, a company or a country will behave, do not read their mission statement. Find out what they get rewarded for.

Take three you meet every day.

Your bank pays you almost nothing on your savings and lends the same money out at many times the rate. The friendly app, the points, the cashback, all of it sits on top of that simple engine. The bank is not evil. It is a business doing what its incentive rewards, which is holding your money for little and lending it for much. Once you see the engine, you read the friendliness for what it is.

Your gym sells you a year up front and hopes you do not come. Its profit depends on members who pay and stay home. The January deal, the long contract, the quiet hope you lose heart by March, none of it is an accident. A gym that needed you to turn up would be built another way.

Your news app does not get paid when you are informed. It gets paid when you stay, scroll and come back. So it learns what keeps you there. What keeps you there is outrage, fear and the argument you cannot resist finishing. The app is doing its job. Its job is your attention, not your understanding.

Here is the part that matters. Seeing incentives is not the same as deciding everyone is a crook. Most people are not scheming. They are swimming downstream, doing what the water rewards, often without noticing the current at all. The bank clerk, the gym owner, the person building the feed, each is a decent enough soul inside a structure that pays for certain moves and not others. Blame the swimmer and you miss the river.

So use the question, but use it clean. When something puzzles you, ask who gains and what they are rewarded for. When advice sounds too confident, ask how the person giving it gets paid. When a system keeps producing a result nobody admits to wanting, look for the reward holding it in place. You will be right more often than you are comfortable being.

The trap to avoid is letting the question curdle into a sneer, deciding the worst of everyone and calling it wisdom. That is not seeing. It is its own kind of blindness. The clear-eyed version holds two things at once. People respond to incentives. Most of them are doing their best inside the ones they were handed. Read the reward, not the person. Then you can change the thing that moves them, which was never the lecture. It was the incentive all along.
