title: "Everyone at Our Fingertips, Nobody in Reach"

My grandparents shared one telephone with the street, near enough. To reach a friend you rang the house. Someone's mother answered. You asked whether you could speak to them. A call was a small event. You thought before you made one. You had something to say.

Now everyone you have ever met sits in your pocket, reachable in a second. Somehow we reach each other less than ever. We have swapped contact for connection and not noticed the exchange. You can have three hundred friends and a quiet phone. You can know what a person had for lunch and not know they are falling apart.

The ease is the problem. When reaching someone took effort, the effort meant something. You wrote the letter, you made the call, you turned up. Now a friendship can run for years on a stream of taps, a thumb on a photo, a one-word reply, the small change of contact that lets you feel in touch while never being in the room. It feels like connection. It is the wrapper with nothing inside.

And the same ease that thins our friendships ends them. Relationships now break over things that would have been nothing in a slower age. A message left on read. A reply that took too long. A photo that did not get a like. We read meaning into the smallest signals, because the small signals are all we have left. We fall out over silences we have invented.

I am not saying throw the phone in the sea. It keeps me close to people I would lose without it. The point is to know the difference between contact and connection and to stop mistaking the first for the second. A heart on a photo is not a phone call. A phone call is not an afternoon. The richer thing always costs more, in time and in nerve. It is always worth it.

So call the person. Not a text, a call, or better, a door. Be the one who makes the effort the ease took away. The people who matter are not at your fingertips. They never were. They are in reach, which is a different thing. Reaching them is the whole of it.
