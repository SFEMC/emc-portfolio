Most of what I write, nobody will ever read. Notes to myself, half-finished thoughts, the same worry turned over on a page until it makes sense or stops mattering. None of it is for an audience. And it is some of the most useful work I do.

We think of writing as a way to tell other people things. You have a finished thought, you write it down, you hand it over. But that is the smaller half of what writing is for. The bigger half is this: you write to find out what you think in the first place. The thought does not exist clean in your head, waiting to be copied out. It is a fog. Writing is how you walk into the fog and force it to take a shape.

You know this feeling. You are sure you understand something until you try to write it down. The pen stops, because the moment you have to put it in order you discover the holes. That is not the writing failing. That is the writing working. It has shown you that you did not understand the thing as well as you felt you did. Speaking lets you fool yourself. The page does not let you.

And the page has another gift. It does not judge. It does not interrupt. It does not get bored of you. You can be petty on it, frightened on it, wrong on it. It takes all of it without a flicker. There are things you cannot say to anyone, not because they are shameful but because saying them out loud makes them too solid. On the page you can look at them sidelong, work them out and decide what to do, in private, with no one watching.

This is why a journal nobody reads is worth keeping. Not as a record, though it becomes one. You keep it because the act of writing the day down makes you notice the day, sort it, feel it and let some of it go. The unexamined day slides past in a blur. The written one, you lived.

So write things no one will read. A notebook, a document, the back of an envelope. Do not wait until you have something worth saying, because the writing is how you find the thing worth saying. The point was never the reader. The point is what the writing does to the writer on the way.
