Say the word propaganda and you picture a man at a microphone in a uniform. Goebbels and his ministry. Stalin and his posters. We keep the word in the past, behind glass, where it belongs to monsters. That is the cleverest thing propaganda ever did, convincing you it died with the regimes that gave it a bad name.

It did not die. It changed its name. In 1928 Edward Bernays, a nephew of Freud, wrote a book called Propaganda and made his living turning its methods on the public for paying clients. He sold cigarettes to women by staging them as torches of freedom. He helped rebrand the whole trade, because after the First World War the word propaganda had soured. The new word was public relations. Same craft, friendlier suit. The art of shaping what a crowd believes did not go to prison with the dictators. It went into advertising and prospered.

It was never theirs alone. During the Cold War the CIA funded a cultural front called the Congress for Cultural Freedom. Through it the agency bankrolled respected magazines, Encounter among them, for over a decade. Readers thought they were reading free thought. Ramparts and the New York Times exposed the arrangement in 1967. A few years later the Church Committee, a Senate inquiry, found around fifty American journalists in secret relationships with the CIA. It found a network of foreign media the agency used to place stories. This is not a rumour from a forum. It is in the public record, investigated by the United States Senate.

So the lineage runs from the ministry to the agency. Now it runs into your pocket. The modern version does not shout. It does not look like propaganda at all, which is the whole reason it works. It looks like your feed. In 2016 a firm called Cambridge Analytica took the data of up to eighty-seven million Facebook users, built psychological profiles and aimed tailored political messages at people based on their fears. State troll farms do the cruder version at scale, flooding the same feeds with invented outrage. The poster on the wall has become a message built for one reader, you, timed for the moment you are most open to it.

You will not get a warning when it reaches you. So you learn to ask three questions of anything that moves you. Who gains if I believe this. What has been left out of the frame. What feeling is it trying to plant. Is that feeling doing my thinking for me. Propaganda has always worked by skipping the reasoning and going straight for the gut. The defence is to put the reasoning back.

The word still belongs to Goebbels and Stalin. The thing belongs to all of us now, to our governments and our advertisers and the platforms we carry everywhere. Spotting it is one of the most useful skills you can build. It is also one of the rarest. The first step is the hardest: accept that you are a target too and that the message made for you is the one you question least.
