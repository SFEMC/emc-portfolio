We treat the ancient world as a dead place. Marble statues with the noses knocked off, names you half remember from school, dates that refuse to stick. Useful for a pub quiz, no use for a Tuesday. That is a mistake, an expensive one. You are living inside their inventions. You cannot read the present without them.

Start with the obvious. The way we vote, argue and hold power to account was worked out in Athens. The way we write law, run courts and bind a state by rules came in large part from Rome. The habit of asking how to live a good life, rather than which god to fear, started with a handful of Greeks who would not stop asking awkward questions. Pull up the floor of almost anything modern and you find them holding it up.

The deeper reason to read them is not the institutions. It is the people. Human nature has not moved an inch. Open Thucydides on a city tearing itself apart and you are reading this morning's news with the names changed. Read the letters of a Roman senator and you meet the same vanity, grief, ambition and fear you saw at work last week. The technology changes. The animal does not. Three thousand years ago a writer in Ecclesiastes looked at it all and said there is nothing new under the sun. He was right. He would be right today.

This matters now more than it has in a long time. The feed sells you the present as if it were brand new, an emergency without precedent, a feeling no one has felt before. The ancients are the cure for that. They show you the pattern: the demagogue who has come round again, the panic that burned out last time too, the empire sure it would last forever. Knowing the pattern does not make you cynical. It makes you steady.

You do not need a degree. Pick one of them and sit with it. Marcus Aurelius wrote a private notebook to keep himself honest. It has outlasted his empire by two thousand years. Start there, a page at a time. You will be surprised how often a man in a toga says the thing you needed to hear and how little, under all our screens and wires, has changed.
