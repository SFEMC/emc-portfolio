There is a phrase the young use to tell someone they have been online too long: touch grass. It is an insult, but it is also the best advice in the world, hiding in a joke. Most of what the feed breaks, the real world mends. The cure costs nothing but stepping outside.

Think about what the screen does to your sense of the world. It tells you people are cruel, the news is all disaster, the other side is a mob, everything is on fire, you are behind and alone. Then you put it down and walk to the shop. The world out there says something different. A stranger holds the door. The trees are doing their slow green thing. A neighbour asks after your mum. The sky is enormous and indifferent and calming. Almost nobody you pass is the cartoon the feed promised. The real world is, for the most part, ordinary and kind. It has been there the whole time you were looking at the other one.

This is not soft advice. It is almost mechanical. Your body was built for the physical world, sunlight, movement, weather, the faces of real people, the feel of a thing in your hands. Feed it a glowing rectangle instead and it reacts the way you would expect, anxious, restless, low, wired and tired at once. A huge amount of modern misery is a body kept indoors and a mind kept online. A surprising amount of it lifts within an hour of grass, air and a horizon further than a wall.

So when it gets too much, as it will, do the dumb obvious thing before the clever ones. Go outside. Walk with no destination. Put your hand on a tree, sit on the ground, look at water if there is any near. Phone the person instead of typing at them. See a friend in the flesh and watch how different it feels from the version of them on a screen.

The feed is a window onto a world built to alarm you. The grass is the world. One of them is real. It is the one you can stand on. Go and touch it.
