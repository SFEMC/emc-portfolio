---
title: "The Forest Was the First System"
collection: "Eyes Wide Open"

---

Long before anyone drew a systems diagram, the forest was already running one. A wood is not a collection of trees. It is a single, breathing machine of feedback and exchange, older than us by a margin that makes our cleverness look very new. We invented systems thinking a few decades ago. The forest has been doing it for four hundred million years.

Look at how it holds itself together. The trees feed the fungi at their roots with sugar. The fungi feed water and minerals back, a trade running underground across a whole wood. When a tree falls, the gap of light it leaves is not a wound. It is an invitation. A dozen waiting seedlings race up into it. Too many deer and the young trees vanish, then the deer starve back to a number the wood can carry. Nothing is in charge. The balance is not designed. It emerges from a million small exchanges, each part doing its own thing, the whole somehow holding.

This is every lesson the other pieces reach for, written in a language older than words. Feedback. Balance. The way a system finds its own level if you leave it alone. And collapse, too, because the forest teaches that part without flinching. Push it past what it can take. Strip enough of it and the whole web unravels faster than anyone expected, because everything was holding everything else up the whole time.

But the forest gives you something the diagrams never can. It is the reason to go and stand in one. Awe. A diagram of a system fits on a page and leaves you cold. A wood at dusk, vast and indifferent and humming with a billion years of working things out, puts you in your place in the kindest way. You are a small, late arrival in something enormous and old that does not need you. That is not a sad thought. It is a relief.

We talk about systems as a skill to learn, a way to get sharper at life. Fair enough. But every so often it helps to remember that we did not invent any of this. We are guests in a world that was running deep, balanced, self-correcting systems before our kind could stand up. The forest never had a manager. It has been managing complexity longer than we have had eyes.

So go and stand under the trees now and then. Not to study them. To remember that the cleverest system you will ever meet grew itself, in the dark, with nobody in charge. It has kept the whole thing in balance since long before the first of us looked up and wondered how.
