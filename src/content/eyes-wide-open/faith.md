I owe a lot to my faith. Some of what I have built I put down to it. On the hard days, maybe the fact that I am still here at all. I am not going to pretend otherwise to sound clever. Faith has carried me.

And here is the part I will not pretend about either. I know my faith is, in large part, an accident. Had I been born three thousand miles east, or four hundred years ago, I would kneel to a different name and feel the same certainty I feel now. The god I reach for is the one my place and my people handed me. That does not make the reaching false. It makes me honest about where my version of it came from.

This is the lottery of birth again, turned on the most personal thing there is. Your faith feels like a truth you found. In the main it is a truth you inherited, the way you inherited your language and your taste in food. None of that is an insult to it. It is the honest map of how belief travels.

What strikes me, once I hold that, is how alike we all are under the different names. The believer, the doubter, the one who would never set foot in a church, almost everyone reaches for something larger than themselves. A god. The universe. Fate. The good. Some order under the noise that means it is not all random and it is not all on you. We are doing the same human thing. We look up. Then we argue over the word for it.

So I hold two things at once. I have stopped feeling I must choose. My faith is real to me and it moves me and I will not apologise for it. And my faith is one window onto something nobody sees whole, drawn by an accident of where I was born. Both true. The certainty and the humility live in the same chest.

There is a practical side to this that holds whether you believe or not. Try to be good. Live as though it matters. If there is a higher power keeping score, you are ready. If there is not, you still spent your one life as a better person, with deeper roots and people who were glad of you. The bet pays either way. I have never understood treating decency as a thing you do when someone is watching and drop when they are not.

So that is where I land. I believe. The believing has held me up. I also know my god wears the clothes of my own time and place. The man across the world, bowing to another name, is reaching for the same thing I am. None of it shakes the faith. It deepens it. A belief big enough to admit it might be one view among many is, I think, a stronger thing than one that has to call everyone else wrong to feel sure.
