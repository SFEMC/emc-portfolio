The Romans worked out something we like to think we are too modern to fall for. The poet Juvenal sneered that you could give the people bread and games and they would hand you everything else without a murmur. Feed them and entertain them and they stop watching what you do. Two thousand years on, the bread is cheap and the circus is in your pocket, running day and night.

There is an older trick stacked on top of it. Divide and rule. A crowd that stands together is dangerous to whoever stands above it. A crowd split into tribes that hate each other is no threat at all, because all that fury has somewhere to go that is not upward. So the people get sorted into teams and pointed at one another. Left at right. The working class at the middle class, one shouting for more tax, the other shouting about who claims benefits. Old at young. One colour at another. Every team certain the other team is the reason their life is hard.

Here is where most people who notice this go wrong. I do not want to. They reach for a villain, a room of men deciding to divide us on purpose. You do not need one. Reaching for one makes you the easiest mark of all, because a clean conspiracy is itself a circus, the most gripping show in town. The truth is quieter and worse. Nobody has to organise it. The division runs on incentives.

Follow them. The feed earns more when you are angry, so it learns to keep you angry. Nothing holds a person like a stranger from the other team being wrong. The politician wins by naming an enemy, so every election hands you one to fear. The outlet keeps you watching by keeping you afraid, so the other half of the country arrives in your living room each night as a threat. Not one of them needs to conspire. Each is a swimmer doing what its current rewards. The current rewards a country at war with itself.

So you get bread and circuses with a modern upgrade. The old version kept you distracted. This one keeps you distracted and split, too busy fighting the family down the road to ask a single question about the people the fighting serves.

Here is the question that breaks the spell. When you feel a clean, easy hatred for a whole group of people, stop and ask who gains from you feeling it. Trace the anger up. Almost never does it lead to the group you were told to blame. It leads to whoever profits from the show. The man you were taught to despise, the one on the other team, is in much the same boat as you, fed the same bread, sold the same circus, told that you are the reason his life is hard. A system that needs you apart has set you against each other.

The way out is not clever. It is almost embarrassing in its simplicity. Refuse the team. Meet the person across the line as a person and watch the cartoon you were handed fall apart. Talk to the neighbour you were told to hate and find the boring, human, overlapping truth: he wants what you want, a decent life and a bit of peace and for his kids to do better than he did. You were never enemies. You were an audience, watching each other instead of the stage.

Keep the bread if you like. But walk out of the circus.
