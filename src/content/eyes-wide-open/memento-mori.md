You are going to die. So is everyone you love. Most of us spend enormous effort not thinking about this. The effort costs us the very thing the thought would give. The old philosophers went the other way. Memento mori, they said. Remember you will die. They kept a skull on the desk, a coin in the pocket, a servant to whisper it in moments of triumph, not to be grim but to wake up.

It sounds morbid until you try it. Then it does the opposite of what you feared. Death is what makes life mean anything. A thing with no end has no shape and no stakes. The reason a moment matters, the reason a person is precious, the reason today does not come again, is that there is a last one of each coming. You do not get told when. Take the ending away and it all goes flat. The clock is what gives the time its value.

Held right, the thought is a knife that cuts the trivial away. Most of what we worry about shrinks the moment you measure it against the fact that you are mortal. The grudge you are nursing. The small humiliation. The thing you are too proud to say. Run them past the plain fact that your time is short and running. Most of them let go of you on their own. You will not waste a precious afternoon on a feud if you remember the afternoons are numbered.

And it points you, hard, at what is real. Ask the question the deathbed asks early, while you can still act on the answer. What would I regret not doing. Who would I want to have called. What am I putting off as though time were endless, when the one thing I do not have is endless time. The people who live well let death sit at the table, not to spoil the meal but to remind them it will end.

So do not look away from it. You do not need a skull on your desk. You need the plain fact, kept close enough to feel: this is finite, all of it, the people and the days and you. That is not a sad truth. It is the thing that makes the morning worth getting up for. You are going to die. So get up and go and live like someone who knows it.
