Most people think one move ahead. Something happens, you picture the result, you decide. The trouble is the world does not stop at the first result. The result has a result. That one has another. The further down the chain you go, the more the real story lives. The people who seem wise are often asking a question the rest of us skip. And then what.

Take the easy examples. You are stressed, so you have a drink. The first effect is you feel better. And then what. You sleep worse, you wake flatter, the stress is still there with a headache on top. A government caps the price of something to help people. The first effect is cheaper bread. And then what. Nobody can afford to make the bread. The shelves empty. The people it was meant to help end up worse off than before.

First-order thinking is fast and feels good. It solves the problem in front of you and hands the cost to next week, who is not in the room to complain. Most of the messes people walk into were not stupid at the first step. They were sensible at the first step and unexamined at the second.

This is the whole reason a quick fix so often makes things worse. The painkiller that lets you keep wrecking the knee. The lie that solves tonight and owns you by Friday. The cheap part that fails in a year. The shortcut at work that doubles the work in a month. Each one wins the first round and loses every round after.

You do not need to see ten moves ahead. You need to see one more than you do now. When you are about to act, run it forward a step. If I do this, what happens next? And after that? Ask it twice and you will catch most of the traps. Ask it three times and you are ahead of almost everyone.

The catch is that second-order thinking feels slower in a world that rewards the fast yes. It will cost you a few easy wins. It will also keep you out of the holes that swallow people who looked one step ahead and no further. Slow is not the price. It is the method.

So before the next decision, small or large, ask the plainest question there is. And then what.
