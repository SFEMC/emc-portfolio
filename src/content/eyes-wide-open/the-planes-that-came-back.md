In the Second World War the Allies had a problem. Too many bombers were not coming home. So they did the sensible thing. They studied the planes that did come back, mapped every bullet hole and looked for the parts taking the most fire. The plan was to add armour where the damage clustered, the fuselage and the wings. More armour where the bullets land. It is hard to argue with.

A statistician named Abraham Wald looked at the same charts and saw the opposite. He worked in a group of mathematicians the war effort leaned on. He asked one question nobody else had. Where are the bullet holes on the planes that did not come back. The charts showed damage all over the wings and body and almost none on the engines. Everyone read that as the engines getting lucky. Wald read it the other way. The planes hit in the engines were not in the hangar to be counted. They were at the bottom of the sea. Armour the engines, he said. Armour the place with no holes, because the holes you are not seeing are the ones that kill.

This is survivorship bias. Once you see it you cannot stop seeing it. You judge by what is in front of you. What is in front of you is the survivors. The planes that came back. The people who made it. The things that worked. The ones that did not are gone, silent, uncounted. They were trying to tell you the most important part.

Look how often the missing planes shape the story. A billionaire tells you he dropped out and followed his gut. A thousand magazines repeat it. You never hear from the thousands who dropped out, followed their gut and sank, because failure does not get a book deal. The advice you read comes from the winners. The winners are the planes that came back. Their tips are the bullet holes on the surviving wings, the damage you can afford to take. The lessons that would save you are on the planes nobody pulled from the water.

It hides in history too. We learn from the empires that left records, the people who won, the buildings that stood. The civilisations that vanished without a trace cannot file a report. So our picture of the past is drawn from the survivors. We mistake it for the whole.

The cure is one habit. When you are handed a clean story about what works, ask where the failures went. Who tried this and sank. What is missing from the chart. Before you copy the survivor, go looking for the planes that did not come back, because they are the ones with something to teach.

Wald saw the holes that were not there. The skill is remembering who is missing from the data you are handed.

*Sources*

- Abraham Wald worked with the Statistical Research Group during the Second World War. He spotted that the bullet-hole charts came from returning aircraft alone and argued for armouring the areas that showed no damage, since planes hit there did not return. Some later details of the story are embellished, but the reasoning is his and is the textbook example of survivorship bias.
