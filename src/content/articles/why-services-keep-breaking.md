Thinking in services is a start. It puts the user at the centre of your decisions. You stop asking "what do we do?" and start asking "what does someone get from us, and how do they get it?" That shift changes what you see, what you measure and who you listen to.

But it doesn't stop there. You can redesign every touchpoint in a journey and still watch the service degrade, because the forces that broke it the first time remain in place. The user-centred view shows you what's broken. It doesn't show you why the organisation keeps producing that outcome.

Thinking in services shows you the problems. Thinking in systems shows you why they keep happening. This article explores that relationship.

## What systems thinking adds

Systems thinking is a holistic way of integrating the service, its customers, its operating environment, its delivery and all the elements that make it happen. You look at these as one connected whole rather than parts to fix in isolation. Service design then works inside that frame, shaping the user-facing parts while staying accountable to everything that sits behind them.

Lou Downe's Good Services sets out fifteen principles for what a good service looks like from the user's perspective. A good service helps someone do something. You can find it. You can understand what it does. You know what to expect. You can complete what you came to do. Those principles give you a standard for the service layer. But a service that meets every one of Downe's principles can still fail if the system around it works against it.

Donella Meadows, in Thinking in Systems, describes how feedback loops, delays and structural relationships between parts drive behaviour that no individual component intended. When you fix a touchpoint without changing the structure that produced the problem, you watch the same failure reappear somewhere else.

You think in services to understand what users experience. You think in systems to understand why.

## What sits around your service

Every service runs inside a system, and that system sits inside others. A university admissions service touches the institutional governance system, the regulatory system, the technology system and the funding system. Each of those connects to systems beyond the organisation: sector regulation, government policy, market conditions. Systems nest inside systems and overlap with each other. The targets teams work to, the governance that decides who owns what, the funding model that shapes where resource goes, the technology that constrains what's possible and the reporting lines that determine who talks to whom all come from different systems acting on the same service at the same time. A service blueprint starts to surface these dependencies, but it rarely traces them far enough to find what drives the behaviour.

When someone maps a user journey, they see the service. When they ask why the journey looks the way it does, they find the system.

Your service is a product of its system. If you want to change the service, you need to understand the system that produces it.

## Four forces in the system that degrade your service

To see how these forces work, take university admissions as an example. A prospective student finds a course, submits an application, provides evidence of their qualifications, receives an offer and enrols. That journey touches marketing, admissions, finance, academic registry, compliance and IT. Each team owns a step. Nobody owns the experience. The pattern applies to any service that crosses team boundaries, but admissions makes it concrete.

### Incentives shape behaviour more than blueprints do

You mapped the admissions journey and found that applicants wait two weeks between submitting their form and hearing anything back. You redesigned the communications, built automated status updates and gave the admissions team a new workflow. Three months later, the wait crept back.

The admissions team shares a processing target with the finance team. Finance needs documents verified before admissions can issue an offer. Finance measures turnaround on verification batches, not on individual applicant timelines. They batch documents weekly because their target rewards throughput, not speed. The admissions team designed for daily handoffs. Finance had no reason to deliver them.

People act according to what the structure around them rewards. When two teams face conflicting targets, each team optimises for its own metric. The handoff between them suffers because nobody gave either team a reason to protect it. No amount of journey mapping fixes this until someone aligns the incentives.

### Missing feedback hides the damage

The same university launches a new online application form. The admissions team measures completed applications and processing times. Both improve. Meanwhile, 35% of applicants abandon the form at the evidence upload step, where the system asks for documents most applicants don't have at that point. Nobody in admissions knows, because abandonment data sits in the digital team's analytics platform. The digital team measures page visits. Admissions measures processed applications. Nobody measures the gap between starting and finishing.

Meadows identifies missing feedback as one of the most common causes of system malfunction. When the people running a service cannot see the full picture of how that service performs, they assume it works. Someone holds the data, but they send it to the wrong place or don't send it at all. When you restore that feedback loop, you shift behaviour more than any process redesign can.

### Workarounds set precedent

The admissions CRM requires staff to log every applicant communication against a predefined category. The categories don't match how the team works, so staff select "general enquiry" for everything and add the real detail in a free text note. That workaround has run for three years. New staff learn it in their first week. Nobody has raised a change request with the CRM vendor because the team solved the problem themselves.

Every exception you tolerate becomes the baseline. Staff build local fixes because the formal process doesn't serve them, and those fixes accumulate into an informal operating model that no documentation describes. The service map shows the design. The workarounds show the reality. Over time, the gap between the two grows until the designed service and the lived service share almost nothing.

### The system rejects good ideas

The Royal Society of Arts calls this the "system immune response." Someone in the admissions team designs a better application route: shorter form, clearer communications, faster decisions. They test it with applicants and staff. The evidence supports it. They present it to the organisation. A procurement officer says the tool they need isn't on the approved list. The academic registry team resists because nobody involved them early enough and the change affects their validation workflow. A senior leader endorses the work in a town hall but won't reallocate resource from a competing priority. The team built the right thing. The organisation couldn't absorb it.

Good service design can survive a resistant stakeholder. It cannot survive a resistant system.

## Where to intervene

Meadows ranks twelve places where you can intervene in a system. Most teams spend their energy at the bottom of that list, where leverage is lowest. Staying with our admissions example, four interventions show why the level matters.

The admissions team reduces their offer turnaround target from 10 working days to 5. Staff work harder, hit it for a few weeks, then drift back. The bottleneck was never the team's speed. Finance still batches document verification weekly. Changing the target didn't change the constraint. That's a parameter adjustment: visible, concrete, low leverage.

The admissions manager gets a new metric added to their weekly report: time from first enquiry to enrolment, not just applications processed. The data shows that applicants who enquire in January but don't apply until March had no contact from the university in between. That doesn't fix anything on its own. It gives someone the evidence to argue for a follow-up communication at the four-week mark. Visibility creates the case for change. A person still has to make the decision. That's an information flow intervention.

A pro-vice-chancellor creates a joint accountability meeting between admissions, finance and registry. Before this, each team reported to a different director and never sat in the same room. Now they review the same applicant timeline data together. When finance's batch schedule causes a two-week delay, everyone can see it. The meeting doesn't solve the problem. It creates the pressure for the teams to solve it together. That's a change to the rules of coordination.

The vice-chancellor decides the institution will measure applicant experience alongside application volume: time to decision, communication quality, completion rate. Teams that previously optimised for their own throughput now share a goal. The incentive structure shifts because the goal shifted. That's the highest-leverage intervention.

Most teams default to the parameter level because it feels concrete and delivers visible results fast. The higher interventions feel slower and more political. They also change what the lower levels produce.

## Two lenses, one practice

Systems thinking doesn't replace service design. You need Downe's principles to stay anchored to what matters: can someone find the service, understand it, use it and get the outcome they need? And you need Meadows' framework to see why the organisation struggles to deliver on those principles despite everyone's best intentions.

When you use one without the other, you either fix symptoms that return or build structural models that forget the person at the end of the journey.

You can start where the first article started: pick one service, map the journey, talk to users and staff. Then ask a different question. Instead of "how do we fix this step?" ask "what in our structure made this outcome predictable?" Follow that question and you'll find incentives, feedback gaps and accumulated workarounds. Those are your real design materials.

Map the service to see what's broken. Read the system to understand why it keeps breaking. Change both and the fix holds.
