You have teams. You have systems. You have processes documented in SharePoint folders that nobody opens. You have projects with start dates, end dates and RAG statuses. You have all the components of delivery. What most organisations don't have is a shared understanding of the services they provide and what it really feels like to use them.

Thinking in services means looking at your organisation from the outside in. Ask "what does someone get from us and how do they get it?" instead of "what is it we do?" That shift changes what you prioritise, what you measure and who you listen to.

If you work in higher education and you haven't made this shift yet, you're designing from the wrong starting point.

## What a service is (and isn't)

A service is something that helps someone do something. Apply for a passport. Admission to a university. Report a change of address. Register a birth. Each of those involves multiple teams, systems and handoffs inside an organisation. The user sees none of that. They see one thing: can I do what I came to do?

A process is what your organisation does internally to deliver that outcome. Processes matter. But when you design around them, you optimise for your own convenience. You create forms that collect what your database needs rather than what the user understands. You build handoffs that make sense on your org chart but leave the user repeating their story to three different teams.

A project has a start and an end. A service doesn't. Admissions runs every cohort. Student finance runs alongside them. Benefits claims run every day. When you treat services as projects, you build something, launch it and move on. Nobody owns what happens next. What you designed drifts because nobody is watching.

## What changes when you think in services

You stop asking "how do we improve this process?" and start asking "does this service work for the people who use it?" Those questions lead to different places.

Process thinking asks: how do we reduce the time it takes to process an application? Service thinking asks: what does the applicant experience from lead through to clicking "submit" and then getting an answer? The first question leads to back-office efficiency. The second leads you to silence, the confusing emails and the phone calls where nobody knows how to help.

Process thinking maps workflows between teams. Service thinking maps the journey the user takes. When you map a workflow, you see the internal steps. When you map a user journey, you see the gaps between those steps where the user waits, repeats themselves or gives up.

Process thinking measures throughput. Service thinking measures completion. You can process 500 applications a week and still have a service that 40% of users abandon halfway through. Throughput tells you the machine is running. Completion tells you whether it actually works.

## Why organisations resist this

Thinking in services requires you to look across boundaries. A student's experience of starting university might span admissions, accommodation, finance, IT, the students' union and their academic department. No single team owns that experience. Nobody has a budget for it. Nobody really reports on it.

Organisations resist because services don't fit the structure. They cross team boundaries, budget lines and reporting hierarchies. A head of admissions can improve the admissions process. Improving the admissions service means working with marketing, finance, IT and academic teams who have their own priorities.

This creates a coordination problem that org charts can't solve. You need someone who can see the whole journey and has the authority (or at least the credibility) to bring those teams together. In government, that person is a service owner. In higher education, that role rarely exists. The work falls to whoever cares enough to do it.

## How to start

Pick one service. Not a system, not a process, not a project. A service. Something a real person uses to get something done. Admissions. Enrolment. Extenuating circumstances. Assessments.

Map the journey from the user's perspective. Start before they reach your system: where do they first find out about the service? What do they need to know before they begin? Then walk through every step. Where do they wait? Where do they get confused? Where do they phone someone because that shiny new digital route you just invested in didn't work?

Talk to the people who run the service. Front-line staff know where it breaks. They know which workarounds hold it together. They know what the documentation says and then what actually happens in practice.

Identify the gaps. Every service has them: handoffs where context gets lost, communications written in institutional language by a senior that has long retired, steps that exist because a system requires them rather than because a user needs them.

Fix one gap. Prove the value. Then do the next one.

## The argument for doing this now

Higher education is competitive in ways that it wasn't ten years ago. Students hold multiple offers and compare experiences. They talk to each other. They notice when one university sends clear, timely communications and another sends automated emails written for a database.

Government faces pressure to deliver more with less. When you generate avoidable contact, force staff to manage workarounds and lose users at step three of a five-step journey, you spend money that better design would save.

In both contexts, the organisations that think in services will outperform the ones that don't. They'll retain more students, convert more applicants, reduce more failure demand and spend less time managing problems they created for themselves.

You don't need a transformation programme to start. You need one service, one map, one conversation with the people who use it. Everything else follows.
