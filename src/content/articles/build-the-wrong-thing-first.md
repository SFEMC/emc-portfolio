In truly agile organisations, prototyping is standard practice. Before you build a service, you test it with users. You create something rough, put it in front of people and find out what works before committing resource to building the real thing.

In many organisations today this does not happen. Teams go from idea to implementation with nothing in between. A committee approves a project. A team builds it. Users experience it. If it does not work, nobody finds out until the complaints come in.

## What prototyping gives you

A prototype is a way of being wrong at low cost. You test an assumption before it costs you six months of development time and a procurement cycle. You learn what users need before you write the specification, not after you have built the system.

Public Digital call this adopting a prototype mindset. The idea is that prototyping is not a phase in a project plan. It is a way of thinking. You test before you commit. You learn before you build.

## Why organisations skip it

Governance cycles reward certainty. Committees want to see a business case with costs, timelines and deliverables before they approve funding. Prototyping sits uncomfortably in that model because the whole point is that you do not know the answer yet.

There is also a capacity problem. Digital teams are small and stretched. The idea of building something you plan to throw away feels wasteful when you have limited time to build the things you are keeping.

Both objections miss the point. Prototyping saves time because you catch problems early. It saves money because you do not build the wrong thing. The cost of not prototyping is the cost of getting it wrong at scale.

## What this looks like in practice

You map the user journey, write user stories and test assumptions with the teams who will use the system. You run it as a controlled pilot with a small group before opening it up. That approach catches problems you would miss in a traditional build: information users need that you had not planned to surface, handoff points between teams that nobody had designed.

Prototyping also bridges the gap between technical teams and the people doing the work on the ground. No jargon, no abstract specifications. Staff can see workflows, interact with them and tell you where they break. That conversation does not happen when you hand someone a requirements document.

## How to introduce prototyping

Start with paper. Sketch a process on a whiteboard and walk a colleague through it. Ask "does this make sense?" before you open a procurement document.

Use the next four to six weeks before a project starts to test your riskiest assumption. If you are building a new service portal, mock up three screens and show them to five users. If you are redesigning an application process, map the current journey and the proposed journey side by side, then ask the people who do the work whether the new version makes sense.

Prototyping does not require new tools or new budgets. It requires a willingness to find out you are wrong before you have spent the money.
