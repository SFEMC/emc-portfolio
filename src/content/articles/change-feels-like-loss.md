Early in my career, a senior leader ran a session on the Kübler-Ross change curve. I don't remember the room. I remember the curve on a screen and the way it sat with me afterwards.

Kübler-Ross described how people process grief. Denial, anger, bargaining, depression, acceptance. The leader applied it to organisational change and something clicked that I've carried through every piece of change work since.

You spend years learning how your job works. You build workarounds for the things nobody fixed. You find shortcuts through the broken bits. You become the person who holds it together. Then someone arrives with a mandate and a timeline and starts asking questions. Why did you do it this way? What was the thinking behind this process?

The intent might be curiosity. You hear judgement. You hear: what you built didn't matter.

Under organisational pressure, leaders push forward. Sometimes they forget to acknowledge what came before. Sometimes they ask questions that feel like interrogation. Either way, the people who held things together hear the same thing: your work wasn't good enough. We're replacing it. We're replacing you.

Nobody says that out loud. Nobody has to. The silence does the work. When you change how someone operates and you skip the part where you thank them for what they built, the silence fills itself. People hear blame where there was none. They hear failure where there was ten years of holding a broken system together with their bare hands.

## The questions that keep people awake

When change arrives, you lie in bed and ask yourself things you would never say at work. Have I been doing it wrong this whole time? Is this my fault? Am I the reason they brought someone in?

And then the question underneath all the others: do they think I'm the problem?

You gave years to this. You came in early and stayed late. You covered for colleagues and picked up the pieces when things broke. You built something out of nothing and made it work. Now someone is standing in front of you with a slide deck explaining why it needs to look different and you're sitting there thinking: was any of it worth anything?

That question burns. It burns because nobody answered it. Nobody stood up before the restructure and said: what you did mattered. We needed you. We still need you. We're changing the system, not discarding the people who ran it.

Leaders try to help. They schedule training sessions, process reviews, capability assessments. They design these to support people through the transition. The person on the receiving end sits in that training room and thinks: they're teaching me how to do my job. The job I've done for years. They think I can't do this. The intent is support. The experience is humiliation. That gap between intent and experience is where organisations lose people for good.

And when the pushback starts, organisations reach for the same playbook. Better emails. More town halls. Another FAQ page. They treat resistance as a communications failure when the problem was never understanding. You understood the change fine. You understood it would cost you something and nobody was willing to name what that something was.

## What I carry from that session

I've spent most of my career embedded with frontline teams. The people who manage and deliver services day to day. The people who hold things together while the organisation decides what comes next.

I've made a career of challenging how things work. I wanted to change things and I wanted to change them fast. I looked at processes and saw inefficiency. I looked at workarounds and saw problems to fix.

What I didn't see, for a long time, were the people who had built those workarounds because nobody gave them anything better. People who had made a broken system function through years of effort and knowledge that lived nowhere except in their heads. People who were now watching me walk in and tell them it wasn't good enough.

I was the person asking those questions. I was the one who forgot to say thank you first.

That Kübler-Ross session changed the question I ask before I start any change work. Not "how do we get people to adopt this?" but "what are they losing and have we named it?"

Sometimes change has to happen fast. Speed doesn't excuse skipping the human part. You still have to sit with the fact that you're asking people to grieve something while you're telling them to move forward. Building trust with a team you're about to disrupt is the hardest thing in this work. Most people skip it because the timeline doesn't feel like it allows for it. The timeline is wrong.

I try to stay conscious of that. Not because I get it right every time. Because the moment I stop thinking about it is the moment I become the person who forgets to say thank you.

The senior leader who stood up and shared that curve on a screen might not even remember the session. I think about it all the time.
