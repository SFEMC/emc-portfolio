Every team says they're user-centred. It's in the strategy document. It's on the wall in the project room. It's in the job descriptions, the sprint goals and the service assessment slides.

Ask that same team to describe the user need their service meets in one sentence, and you'll get silence. Or a vague answer about "improving the experience." Or a description of what the service does rather than what the user needs it to do.

The label has become a badge, not a practice. Teams wear it without doing the work it demands.

## How the label gets misused

User-centred design has a specific meaning. You start with the needs of the people who will use the service. You research those needs. You design around them. You test your design with real users. You iterate based on what you learn. The user's needs drive decisions.

In practice, many teams reverse the order. They decide what to build, then find users to validate the decision. They run research sessions but ignore findings that conflict with the plan. They design around system constraints or organisational preferences and call it "user-centred" because they spoke to some users along the way.

This isn't malicious. Teams are under pressure to deliver. Stakeholders have expectations. Budgets are fixed. Timelines are tight. "We'll do user research" becomes "we'll do a survey." "We'll test with users" becomes "we'll demo to stakeholders." The language stays. The practice erodes.

## The difference between saying it and doing it

Teams that practise user-centred design can point to specific decisions they changed because of research findings. They can show you where the design moved away from the original plan because users struggled with it. They can describe trade-offs they made between what the organisation wanted and what users needed.

Teams that wear the label but don't practise it describe their process in terms of outputs: "we did ten research sessions," "we ran three rounds of usability testing." Outputs without outcomes. Activity without impact. The research happened. It didn't change anything.

The test is simple. Look at the last five design decisions the team made. How many of them were informed by user research? How many changed direction because of what they learned? If the answer is zero, the team isn't user-centred. It's user-aware at best.

## Why the distinction matters

When "user-centred" loses its meaning, it loses its power. It becomes a box to tick rather than a standard to meet. Teams stop challenging decisions that don't serve users because everyone has agreed they're "user-centred" already. The label provides cover for business-as-usual.

This hurts users because the services they interact with get designed around organisational convenience, wrapped in user-centred language. It hurts teams because they miss the value that genuine user-centred practice delivers: services that work first time, reduce support costs and build trust.

## What to do about it

Stop using the label as a description and start using it as a standard. Define what user-centred means for your team in concrete terms. Which decisions require user research before they're made? What evidence do you need before you move from one phase to the next? How do you handle a conflict between what users need and what the organisation wants?

Make the practice visible. Show stakeholders the research findings, the design changes and the reasons behind them. When a decision goes against user needs, say so. Name the trade-off. Record it. Come back to it.

Hold each other to account. When someone says "we're user-centred," ask: what did you change because of what users told you? If the answer is nothing, the label isn't earned.
