Having moved from delivering digital products and services in UK Central Government into Higher Education, I expected the two to feel similar. Public-facing users, legacy systems, complex stakeholder landscapes, heavy regulation and scrutiny over spending.

In practice, they feel like completely different worlds.

In government, you have the Service Standards, GDS patterns, a shared language around user needs and delivery phases. The guardrails exist and the challenge is moving at pace within them.

In higher education, those frameworks don't seem to exist. You're building the case for user-centred design at the same time as trying to do it. The appetite is there, but the structures, governance, and shared vocabulary around digital and service delivery are still catching up.

## A few things I've noticed working across both

Government teams tend to underestimate how much autonomy they have. University teams tend to overestimate how much buy-in they have.

Government has a defined Service Owner role and delivery teams have set roles and responsibilities within DDaT and Service Design and Delivery functions. In universities, those lines are blurred. One person might cover product, delivery, and stakeholder management without any of those originally being their job description or ample time given to do it right.

"Discovery" means something specific in government. In higher education, you might find yourself explaining why an extra four to six weeks is needed to do it properly.

## What Higher Education has that government doesn't

University teams pilot new tools and approaches without waiting for a central mandate. A lecturer starts using AI in their teaching, a faculty team builds a student-facing service. They adopt a test and learn approach by instinct, not because a framework told them to. The challenge is ensuring those efforts align to policy, are joined up and repeatable when they do work.

University teams also sit closer to their end users than most government teams ever will. Academics, Professional Services and Student Support Staff interact with their users every day. Government teams invest in recruitment, access panels and incentive schemes to recreate that proximity through research. Universities have it built in.

## What Higher Education can learn from Government

Government isn't perfect, but one thing it does well is work in the open. GDS and its community publish blogs, week notes and delivery updates as standard practice. Teams write about what worked, what didn't and what they're still figuring out. They share mistakes and challenges, not just successes. I sat in communities of practice and had access to groups of people doing the same thing I was doing across the country, people I could reach out to and lean on. Teams share resources across departments as standard. That kind of peer network looks to be rare in Higher Education although it does feel like it's starting to shift as organisations like Salesforce enter the sector and bring their own community infrastructure with them.

Higher education doesn't have that culture yet. Digital teams work quietly, good practice stays locked inside individual institutions and teams across the sector end up solving the same problems in isolation. Working in the open, sharing what you're learning, treating test and learn as a way of operating rather than a phase: the sector could benefit from all of it.

Both sectors are heavily regulated and that regulation can slow delivery down. In government, delivery teams work within established frameworks for managing that tension. In higher education, governance structures can stall progress while teams wait for the right sign-off or the right committee cycle. When things go wrong during a test and learn approach, delivery and service teams take the blame. Shifting to a culture where learning from failure is expected, not punished, requires buy-in that goes beyond any delivery team.

The transformation challenges in higher education feel like the ones I first encountered in government as it was starting out on its journey. Government has had years to refine its approach to service design, delivery, and working at scale. That practice exists, and Higher Education providers can lean into it rather than starting from scratch.

Neither sector has it figured out. But the conversations are starting to overlap, and I think both sides have something to learn from the other.
