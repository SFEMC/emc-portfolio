Every organisation delivers services. The insurance company that processes a claim. The council that issues a parking permit. The university that enrols a student. The retailer that handles a return. Whether you use the word or not, you run services that people depend on.

Most organisations treat these as processes. They map internal workflows, cut handoff times, automate steps. The work helps. But it answers the wrong question. Process improvement asks how your team works. Service design asks whether the person on the other end got what they needed.

## The gap between improving a process and designing a service

A customer contacts your support team. They explain the problem. Your team logs it, assigns it, resolves it. You measure average handling time. You track how fast the ticket moves through the queue.

But the customer had to find the right contact channel first. They repeated their details to two different people. They received an automated email that contradicted what the agent told them. They waited four days without knowing whether anyone was working on it. Your process ran fine. The service failed.

Service design starts with the person. You map the end-to-end journey across every team, system and channel they touch. You find the gaps between departments where people fall through. You ask what the user needs at each stage and whether your organisation delivers it.

When you do this, you stop optimising fragments and start designing something whole.

## Government proved this works at scale

The UK Government Digital Service built the GOV.UK Service Standard: 14 points that every public service must meet. Understand user needs. Solve a whole problem for users. Provide a joined-up experience across channels. Make the service accessible. Hundreds of teams across government now follow the same standard. Departments that had never coordinated on design started using common language and shared principles.

Lou Downe, who led service design at GDS, published 15 principles of good service design that apply far beyond government. A good service is findable. It sets expectations. It requires no prior knowledge to use. It works for everyone. These principles hold whether you run a hospital, a bank, a university or a logistics company.

Public Digital, founded by several of the people behind GDS, have written about how design thinking shapes organisational performance. Their book *Competitive Advantage by Design* makes the case that organisations who treat design as a core capability outperform those who bolt it on as an afterthought.

## Service standards and service objectives

A service standard tells you how to build and run services. It sets the rules that apply across your organisation: understand user needs, design for accessibility, provide a joined-up experience, iterate based on evidence. You write it once and every team follows it.

Service objectives are different. They describe what each individual service commits to delivering for its users. Your claims service might commit to keeping customers informed at every stage. Your onboarding service might commit to new users completing setup without needing to contact support. Each service defines its own objectives shaped by what its users need.

The standard gives you consistency. The objectives give each team something specific to measure against and improve. Together, they move you beyond internal efficiency into something more useful: a way to define, track and improve the experience for the person on the other end.

## Marketing teams should own brand across services

Most organisations have a marketing team that controls brand, tone and messaging for customer acquisition. Marketing teams have content designers already built in. They write for users, structure information and maintain consistency as part of their day job. But their remit often stops at the point of sale. Once someone becomes a customer, the language changes. Emails come from systems nobody in marketing has reviewed. Letters use templates written by teams with no design support. Different departments adopt different tones.

The capability to fix this already sits inside the organisation. Extend marketing's remit beyond acquisition into the services customers use every day. If the brand promise says you are clear, responsive and easy to deal with, that promise should hold in a service update email and a billing letter, not only on the website.

When one team owns language and brand across the full service landscape, users get a coherent experience. When nobody owns it, every department invents its own tone and the organisation speaks with twenty different voices.

## The shift

Process improvement asks: how do we make this workflow faster? Service design asks: can this person do what they came to do without confusion, repetition or being passed between teams?

When you move from the first question to the second, you change what gets measured. You stop tracking internal cycle times and start tracking whether users completed what they needed to. You stop treating each department as an island and start mapping the journeys that cross between them. You stop building systems for staff convenience and start building services for the people they exist to serve.

Every organisation has the raw material for this. You have teams who deliver things to people. You have users who can tell you what works and what doesn't. The question is whether you design those experiences deliberately or let them emerge by accident.
