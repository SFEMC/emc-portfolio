If you're like me, it sometimes feels like you spend your entire working life switching between contexts, teams and priorities. A new policy lands. A team restructures. You move somewhere new and spend the first month learning a different language for the same problems.

Last year I stepped out of the familiarity of government and into higher education - a new place with the same level of scrutiny and regulation but admittedly an awful lot to learn.

We have more access to information than at any point in history. Articles, research, podcasts, reports, LLMs at your fingertips - all of it a tap away, all of it arriving faster than you can absorb it. The challenge stopped being access a long time ago. The challenge now is keeping hold of what matters, finding it again when you need it and making connections across everything you've taken in.

A few months ago I stumbled across an article about Obsidian that intrigued me. Three months later and I already consider it the single most useful tool in my current workflow and one I recommend to anyone doing knowledge-heavy work.

A colleague then pointed me to Tiago Forte's Building a Second Brain and his CODE framework - Capture, Organise, Distill, Express. Reading it, I realised the system I'd been building mapped closely to what Forte describes. But it also showed me where I could improve. I used his thinking to sharpen how my vault is structured and how I move from capturing information to producing something useful with it.

## Why Obsidian?

Obsidian stores everything as plain Markdown files on your local desktop. No cloud dependency. No subscription lock-in for your own thinking. Your files sit on your machine, under your control - which matters if you handle sensitive or regulated work. I'd encourage anyone building a system like this to think about GDPR and data protection from day one. Because your vault lives locally, you choose what goes in and what stays out.

## What I built

My vault has evolved into nine areas. Each one solves a different problem.

**Clippings** - Obsidian has a Chrome plugin that lets you save articles, highlights and passages directly into your vault with metadata attached. I use Obsidian Sync to extend this to my phone, so I can clip something useful from wherever I am. This replaced the old habit of bookmarking things I'd never look at again.

**Pocket** - Pocket is a voice-to-text capture tool that transcribes your spoken thoughts and structures them as per your own set of rules. I use it as a personal thinking space - not for meetings or anything involving other people, but for my own reflections. I run morning briefs and end-of-day debriefs through it. I capture random ideas and half-formed thoughts, most of which arrive when I'm trying to relax, on a walk or getting ready for bed. Before Pocket, those midnight ideas would become an email to myself or a random message to a colleague on Teams (sorry about that, by the way). Now they land in my vault, timestamped and searchable. I even started reading with my Pocket running to capture details relevant to my current work as I go.

**Synthesis** - This is where raw material becomes structured thinking. It takes all of my inputs and writes synthesis notes that connect ideas across sources. A pattern from one piece of research might echo something I spotted in a different context. Synthesis notes make those connections visible.

**Weeknotes** - Every week I write a short personal note covering what happened, what I learned and what I'm turning over. Obsidian has its own daily and weekly note features, but I prefer my own format. I keep my weeknotes private - they aren't for sharing. But they've proved valuable when I need to trace how my thinking has developed, or when I'm writing something that draws on a longer arc of work.

**Notes** - General-purpose notes. Quick references, scratch thinking, things that don't fit neatly elsewhere. Low ceremony, high utility.

**Articles** - Drafts, ideas and finished pieces all live here. Keeping them in the vault means I can link back to the sources and synthesis notes that fed into them, even at the earliest sketch stage.

**Thinking Partner** - I've built and moulded an AI thinking partner over time to pressure-test ideas, explore questions and work through problems. The outputs get stored in my vault with context about the question I was wrestling with. This means I can revisit previous thinking rather than starting the same conversation from scratch.

**Extended Research** - I set up a workflow that takes what I add to my vault, researches credible sources around it, annotates what I've written and feeds relevant material back. Sometimes that supports what I'm actively working on. Sometimes it adds depth to existing material without me lifting a finger.

**PDFs** - A straightforward store for useful documents: books, research papers, frameworks. Obsidian indexes them so I can search across everything in one place.

## The workflows that tie it together

The vault is useful on its own, but the workflows are what turned it from a folder of notes into a system I use every day. I've set up scheduled automations that synthesise new material, surface connections between notes I wouldn't have spotted manually and feed research back into existing work. My ideas and thinking aren't just saved and findable - they're always growing. Material I added weeks ago gets enriched, connected and resurfaced as new inputs arrive. The system works even when I'm not actively tending it.

Mapping this back to Forte's CODE framework: Clippings, Pocket and PDFs handle the capturing. The vault structure handles organising. Synthesis and Weeknotes handle distilling. Articles and my thinking partner handle expressing. Where I've gone further is in automating parts of the organising and distilling, so the system keeps working in the background.

## A note on data protection

Obsidian sits on your desktop. That's a strength, but it's also a responsibility. I keep personal data and anything identifiable out of my vault entirely. This is a thinking and knowledge tool, not a filing system for sensitive material. If you build something similar, consider your obligations under GDPR and your organisation's data policies before you start filling it.

## What changed for me

I have easy access to everything I've read, thought and written - all in one place, all searchable, all connected. The gap between "I read something about this" and finding it again dropped from hours to seconds.

My writing improved because I build on previous thinking rather than starting from scratch. The synthesis layer means every new piece draws on a connected body of prior work.

But the power of what I've created extends further than retrieval. The system compounds. Every week I use it, the connections between ideas grow denser and the material gets richer. The research workflow actively extends what I've captured - pulling in credible sources, annotating what I've written and feeding new depth back into the vault without me asking it to. Three months of inputs are already producing outputs I didn't anticipate when I saved the original material.

It changed how I think, not just how I store things. I notice patterns earlier. I ask better questions because I can see what I've already explored. I build on previous reasoning instead of circling back to the same starting point.
