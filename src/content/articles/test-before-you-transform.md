Most transformation programmes follow the same pattern. A board approves a vision. A team writes a business case. Procurement starts. Technology gets built. Then someone switches it on and hopes the organisation can absorb the change.

When it does not work, the instinct is to blame adoption. Staff resisted. Users did not engage. The problem is rarely resistance. The problem is that nobody tested whether the new way of working made sense before committing to it at scale.

## Test and learn is not optional

Public Digital describe test and learn as an enduring, cyclical way of working: test, learn, test, learn. The principle is simple. Anything that works at scale grew from something that worked at a smaller scale first. You start narrow, learn from real users in real conditions and scale up based on evidence.

Most transformation programmes do the opposite. Teams design the target state, build it over 18 months and roll it out across the organisation in one go. When the new process hits reality, it breaks in ways nobody predicted because nobody tested it with the people doing the work.

Test and learn changes that sequence. You test your riskiest assumptions first. You learn what works before you write the specification. You treat every phase of transformation, from strategy through to live operations, as something you can iterate on.

## Model offices make test and learn practical

The concept sounds straightforward. In practice, organisations struggle to apply it because they have nowhere safe to test. A model office gives you that space.

A model office is a controlled environment where you run the new process with a small group before rolling it out. You take a team, give them the proposed workflow, the proposed tools and the proposed governance, then watch what happens. You measure how long tasks take, where handoffs break down, what information staff need that you had not thought to provide.

You are not testing the technology. You are testing whether the whole service works: the process, the people, the data, the decisions. When you find problems (and you will find them), you fix them before they reach the wider organisation.

This is cheaper than fixing a failed rollout. It is faster than running a parallel change programme six months after launch and it builds confidence across the organisation because staff can see the new way of working before anyone asks them to adopt it.

## Why this step gets skipped

Organisations reward certainty. Business cases need a fixed scope, a fixed timeline and a fixed budget. Testing sits uncomfortably in that model because the whole point is that you do not yet know the answer.

There is also a pace problem. Showing progress matters and standing up a model office can feel like slowing down. It is not. Running a four-week pilot with one team saves you the six months you would spend fixing what went wrong if you had skipped it.

The cost of not testing is the cost of getting it wrong at scale.

## Where to start

Pick your riskiest process. Staff the model office with the people who will do the work in the real world, not a project team. Run it for four to six weeks. Measure what works, fix what does not and scale from there.

Transformation is a people-led design problem, not a technology project. If you treat it like one, test before you commit and learn before you build, you give yourself the best chance of delivering something that works for the people experiencing it and the teams managing it.
