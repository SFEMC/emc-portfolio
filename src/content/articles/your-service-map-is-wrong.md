Every delivery team has a map on the wall. Post-its in neat rows. A clean journey from left to right. Start here, do this, end there. It looks good in a show-and-tell.

It's also wrong.

Not because the team didn't do the work. They did. They researched, they mapped, they validated. But they mapped the happy path: the journey where everything goes right. The user knows what to do. The system works. Nobody falls through the cracks.

That's not where the service lives. The real service lives in the exceptions, the escalations and the workarounds that never make it onto the wall.

## The happy path problem

Happy paths are useful. You need to know what the intended journey looks like. But designing a service around the happy path is like designing a road network and assuming every driver will follow the signs and stick to the speed limit.

In practice, users take wrong turns. They submit incomplete information. They misunderstand what's being asked. They have circumstances the form doesn't account for. They phone a contact centre and describe a problem that doesn't match any of the scripts.

When that happens, the service improvises. A caseworker makes a judgement call. Someone sends an email to a colleague asking what to do. A manager creates a spreadsheet to track the cases that don't fit the process. These improvised responses become the real service for the people who need them most.

## What's missing from the map

Most service maps don't show what happens when things go wrong. They don't show the error states, the manual interventions, the decisions that staff make outside the defined process. They don't show the time a user spends waiting because their case doesn't fit a standard pathway.

They also don't show the workarounds. Every service has them. Shared spreadsheets that track exceptions. Email chains that substitute for a proper case management system. Informal agreements between teams about how to handle edge cases. These workarounds are invisible on the map but load-bearing in practice.

If your map doesn't include the workarounds, it doesn't show how the service runs. It shows how you wish it ran.

## How to fix it

Map the unhappy paths. For every step in the user journey, ask: what happens when this goes wrong? What does the user do? What does the staff member do? Where does the case go? Who picks it up?

Talk to the people running the service. Front-line staff know where the map breaks down because they deal with it every day. They know which cases take ten times longer than expected. They know which workarounds are holding the service together. They know what the documentation doesn't cover.

Map the workarounds as part of the service, not as exceptions to it. If a spreadsheet is tracking cases that the system can't handle, that spreadsheet is part of the service. If a team lead is making routing decisions in their head because the process doesn't account for a common scenario, that decision-making is part of the service.

Once you've mapped the real service, you can start designing it properly. Some workarounds need to be built into the system. Some exceptions need defined pathways. Some edge cases need to be accommodated rather than ignored.

## The test

Look at your service map. Ask yourself: does this show what happens to the person whose case doesn't fit? Does it show the spreadsheet that Tracy uses to track the difficult ones? Does it show what happens when the API is down and someone has to process things manually?

If the answer is no, your map shows the service you designed. It doesn't show the service you're running and the gap between those two things is where your users are having the worst experience.
